Breadfruit is common in Sri Lanka and one way they eat it is in a thick and rich coconut curry (Del Curry). Here's my take on how to make this simple dish. The Breadfruit is traditionally boiled with spices, then coconut milk is added to reduce, then topped with a temper. For a firmer texture, I bake the breadfruit with spices first. I'll also add a recipe for the mock meat fry I had on the side. 

**Del curry** (serves 3)

- 1 Breadfruit (unripe)
- 2 tsp curry powder
- 1 tsp turmeric powder
- 1 tsp fenugreek seeds
- 1 tsp chili powder
- 1 tsp pepper
- 4 cloves garlic
- 2 sprigs curry leaves
- 1 pandan stalk halved (optional)
- 2 tbsp oil
- 1 medium (or larger) red onion
- 3 dried red chili (or flakes)
- 1 tbsp brown mustard seeds (subs. with yellow or djon mustard)
- 1/2 can coconut milk

1. Cut the top of bottom of the breadfruit. Peel with a knife, ensuring NO green remains on the breadfruit. Cut into quarters lengthways and remove the core. Some seeds remaining are fine. Then, cut each quarter in half lengthways and cut into pieces about 2-3cm in thickness.
2. Soak in salty water for 20m
3. Pat dry and toss with 1 tbsp oil, curry powder, turmeric, chili, and pepper. Bake in a preheated oven/airfry at 200 until outside is golden or edges slightly charred (20-30m in oven, 15-20 in airfrier)
4. In a pot, boil a cup of water with 1 sprig curry leaves, half pandan, fenugreek, peeled and crushed garlic, and a pinch of salt.
5. Add the baked breadfruit and coconut milk to the pot, boil to reduce until thickened. Taste for salt
6. In a pan on medium heat, add oil and thinly sliced onions. Toss until browned.
7. Once brown, add the remaining curry leaves and pandan and mustard seeds. Toss until mustard seeds start popping
8. Add this temper to the pot and mix well. Serve hot with rice and other sides

**Notes**

1. You can use any curry powder you have on hand. Sri-Lankan curry powder may be difficult to source. There is a Sri-Lankan store here in Brisbane, but I did not visit it
2. Unripe breadfruit should be mostly firm and may leak a white substance. Have paper towels on hand to wipe them away, and coat your hands and knife in oil to avoid stickiness.
3. The exterior of the breadfruit is extremely bitter, it may seem wasteful to keep peeling until there is no green but this should be done to avoid a bitter stew. I made this mistake T__T
4. If the breadfruit is slightly ripe, it can be difficult to work with and may turn to mush. It will still taste nice! When fully ripe though, it will be quite custardy and is probably best eaten as is. There's definitely recipes out there using ripe breadfruit
5. You can boil the breadfruit in the pot with all the spices, skipping the soaking and baking step. This is the standard/traditional approach. Baking gives a drier and firmer breadfruit, while boiling would make it softer. You should boil it with an excess of water and stir to ensure it doesn't stick to the bottom. 
6. The mixture of hot oil with bloomed spices and fresh items to be added to a wet dish is called a Temper. This is common in South Asian cooking. A similar process is used in southern Chinese cooking, where fresh herbs are placed on top of a stew and hot oil is dunked on top.

**Mock meat fry**

1. Prepare mock meat. This may be draining and peeling from a can, or microwaving from frozen depending on whichever you have access too. Use about 200g
2. Stir fry 1 sliced onion on medium-high heat with 1 tbsp yellow lentils/split peas (eg. Urad Dal, Mung Dal, Yellow split peas)
3. When browned, add 3 chopped and diced garlic, equal amount peeled and diced ginger, 1 sprig worth of curry leaf, 1/4 pandan stalk (optional) and 1 sliced chili
4. Stir until fragrant, then add 1 tsp Hing/Asafoetida, 1 tsp mustard seeds, a few cracks of pepper, and 1 tsp dark soy for color (optional)
5. Add the mock meat and stir until coated. Add some salt and stir. Taste and adjust.
6. Deglaze any stuck bits with a little water and serve.
